Newton's Method for Optimization

Now, suppose we wish to minimize a function f:R→Rf : \R \rightarrow \Rf:R→R. The standard approach looks for stationary points satisfying the first-order condition f′(x)=0f'(x) = 0f′(x)=0. If fff is convex (for example), then there is a unique stationary point at the global minimum of fff.

To find a stationary point, we can apply Newton's method to find roots of the derivative f′:R→Rf' : \R \rightarrow \Rf′:R→R mapping x↦f′(x)x \mapsto f'(x)x↦f′(x).

xt+1≡xt−f′(xt)f′′(xt) x_{t+1} \equiv x_t - \frac{f'(x_t)}{f''(x_t)} xt+1​≡xt​−f′′(xt​)f′(xt​)​

So, the connection between Newton's method for optimization and root finding in one dimension is clear. However, in two dimensions, Newton's method for optimization looks like

xt+1≡xt−(∇xt2f)−1(∇xtf) x_{t+1} \equiv x_t - (\nabla^2_{x_t} f)^{-1} (\nabla_{x_t} f) xt+1​≡xt​−(∇xt​2​f)−1(∇xt​​f)

Multivariate Newton's Method for Optimization

Now, suppose we want to minimize the multivariate function f:Rn→Rf : \R^n \rightarrow \Rf:Rn→R.

  • The derivative evaluated at any x∈Rnx \in \R^nx∈Rn is a linear transformation ∇xf:Rn→R\nabla_x f : \R^n \rightarrow \R∇x​f:Rn→R
    • Can be represented by a row vector.
  • At a critical point, the function ∇xf\nabla_x f∇x​f should map every input to zero.
    • i.e. at a critical point ∇xf\nabla_x f∇x​f is represented by the zero vector.
Problem: Derive a multivariable version of Newton's method for finding critical points of f:Rn→Rf : \R^n \rightarrow \Rf:Rn→R.
  • (Use the linear approximation interpretation of Newton's root-finding method.)
  • What assumptions are needed about the function fff for this method to work?

Solution

Essentially, we want to find zeros of the map φ:Rn→Rn\varphi : \R^n \rightarrow \R^nφ:Rn→Rn mapping x↦∇xfx \mapsto \nabla_x fx↦∇x​f. The linear approximation to φ\varphiφ at a point xt∈Rnx_t \in \R^nxt​∈Rn is ψ(y)=φ(xt)+(∇xtφ)(y−xt) \psi(y) = \varphi(x_t) + (\nabla_{x_t} \varphi) (y-x_t) ψ(y)=φ(xt​)+(∇xt​​φ)(y−xt​)

Since φ:Rn→Rn\varphi : \R^n \rightarrow \R^nφ:Rn→Rn, its derivative φ∈L(Rn→Rn)\varphi \in L(\R^n \rightarrow \R^n)φ∈L(Rn→Rn) can be represented by an (n×n)(n \times n)(n×n) matrix. Assume this matrix is invertible. Choose xt+1x_{t+1}xt+1​ to be a root of ψ\psiψ,

ψ(xt+1)=0  ⟹  (∇xtφ)(xt+1−xt)=−φ(xt)  ⟹  xt+1−xt=−(∇xtφ)−1φ(xt) \begin{aligned} \psi(x_{t+1}) = 0 &\implies (\nabla_{x_t} \varphi) (x_{t+1}-x_t) = -\varphi(x_t) \\ &\implies x_{t+1} - x_t = - (\nabla_{x_t} \varphi)^{-1} \varphi(x_t) \\ \end{aligned} ψ(xt+1​)=0​⟹(∇xt​​φ)(xt+1​−xt​)=−φ(xt​)⟹xt+1​−xt​=−(∇xt​​φ)−1φ(xt​)​

Notice that ∇xtφ\nabla_{x_t} \varphi∇xt​​φ is the Hessian of fff, and φ(xt)\varphi(x_t)φ(xt​) was defined to be the gradient. Therefore, multivariate Newton's method for optimization has the form

xt+1=xt−(∇xt2f)−1(∇xtf) x_{t+1} = x_t - (\nabla^2_{x_t} f)^{-1} (\nabla_{x_t} f) xt+1​=xt​−(∇xt​2​f)−1(∇xt​​f)
